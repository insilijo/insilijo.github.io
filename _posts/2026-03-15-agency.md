## Core Thesis

Agency is not an intrinsic personal property in institutional life; it is allocated.

Possible sharper thesis:
Strategy is partly the design of agency under constraint.

## Opening Example

Choose one:

1. Scientist who "owns" a program but cannot move budget, headcount, or scope.
2. Product manager responsible for outcomes but surrounded by veto points.
3. Junior employee encouraged to show initiative but only within narrow, invisible limits.

Goal:
Establish the difference between nominal ownership and real capacity to change outcomes.

## Main Argument

Agency is not merely the ability to act.
Agency is the ability to act with consequence.

Points to develop:

1. Institutions manufacture action by allocating decision rights, information, legitimacy, and protection.
2. Responsibility is often assigned more broadly than authority.
3. "Empowerment" language often masks a system that wants initiative without surrendering control.

## Connection to Earlier Trilogies

Tie explicitly to prior work:

1. Ontologies determine how the system is described.
2. Boundaries determine what the system is allowed to see.
3. Agency determines who inside the system is allowed to convert description and perception into action.

Possible line:
A system is not just a frame around reality; it is a selective engine that turns some people into actors and others into substrate.

## Strategy Angle

Make the positioning explicit here.

Claims to build:

1. Strategy is not only markets, products, and competition.
2. Strategy also determines where decisions can be made and who can reinterpret ambiguity.
3. Many organizational failures labeled "culture" or "communication" are actually failures of agency design.

## Failure Modes

Outline the cost of weak or fake agency:

1. Delay
2. Decision bottlenecks
3. Learned helplessness
4. Moral evasion
5. "Ownership" as exposure rather than discretion

Strong line to preserve:
A person with nominal responsibility but no practical ability to alter outcomes does not possess meaningful agency. They possess exposure.

## Technical Organizations Section

Focus on how agency changes in specialized or technical systems.

Points:

1. As specialization increases, fewer people can inspect the whole system directly.
2. Review layers, dashboards, process, and standards mediate action.
3. The people who control translation layers often have more agency than the people doing nominal delivery.

## Diagnostic Questions

Potential list for the essay:

1. What are they allowed to decide?
2. What resources can they move?
3. What consequences can they impose or absorb?
4. What information reaches them in time?
5. What happens when they disagree upward?

## Ending

Land on the practical point:

1. Agency is a design problem, not a motivational slogan.
2. Organizations that cannot locate real agency near reality become ceremonial.
3. The strategic question is not whether people are "empowered" in principle, but whether the system has concretely decided who is allowed to change reality.
